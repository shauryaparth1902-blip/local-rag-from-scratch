Engineering Mechanics: Friction is a force that resists the relative motion of solid surfaces. The maximum static friction force is equal to the coefficient of static friction multiplied by the normal force. It prevents objects from sliding down an incline.

Applied Chemistry: Covalent bonds involve the sharing of electron pairs between atoms. These electron pairs are known as shared pairs or bonding pairs, and the stable balance of attractive and repulsive forces between atoms is essential for creating molecules like water (H2O).

Astronomy: Stars evolve over millions or billions of years. When a star runs out of nuclear fuel, its core collapses. For massive stars, this collapse can cross the Chandrasekhar limit, leading to a massive explosion called a supernova, leaving behind a neutron star or a black hole.

When a star finally exhausts its nuclear fuel, its ultimate fate is dictated primarily by its mass, a concept fundamentally tied to the Chandrasekhar limit. For average stars, the core collapses under gravity until electron degeneracy pressure halts the implosion, leaving behind a dense, glowing ember known as a white dwarf. However, if the collapsing core exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit of approximately 1.44 solar masses, this pressure is no longer sufficient to counteract the crushing gravitational forces. This triggers a catastrophic collapse, resulting in a spectacular supernova explosion that scatters newly forged heavy elements across the cosmos. The remnants of these incredibly massive celestial bodies face an even more extreme destiny. If the remaining core mass is immensely high, it will continue to collapse infinitely until it forms a singularity, creating a black hole from which not even light can escape. The boundary surrounding this singularity is known as the event horizon, a region where the standard laws of theoretical physics break down and exotic quantum mechanics concepts, such as the slow emission of Hawking radiation, begin to take over.
